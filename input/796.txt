"></picture></figure><header class="app-banner-header middle"><div class="banner-intro"><h1 class="banner-title">Everything We Learned in One Year About Thousands of Years of Human Evolution</h1><h2 class="banner-dek">Seven breakthroughs rewrote our history.</h2></div><div class="article-info"><div class="article-info__page"><address class="article-author">By <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/user/45-sarah-sloat">Sarah Sloat</a></address> on <time class="app-time" datetime="2018-01-04T15:16:00.000000Z"><a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/archive/january/2018/science">January 4, 2018</a></time></div><div class="article-info__labels">Filed Under <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/ancient-peoples">Ancient Peoples</a>, <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/bones">Bones</a>, <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/death">Death</a>, <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/evolution">Evolution</a>, <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/genetics">Genetics</a>, <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/history">History</a> & <a class="app-link" href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/sex">Sex</a></div></div></header></div><section class="article-card flex palm-flex-column app-content"><div class="article-column flex styles--2"><div class="alignment alignment--top alignment--middle"><div class="alignment__body"><p class="-first clearfix contained"><span class="dropcap">I</span>f you were to encounter humans from 200,000 years ago, you might be struck by how much they look like humans in 2018. Anatomically modern and large-brained, with faces and teeth not dissimilar to our own, these early <em>Homo sapiens</em> went to war, formed relationships, and created tools, just like us. There’s still plenty to learn about our direct evolutionary ancestors, but this year, thanks to the widespread use of genomic sequencing, scientists gave us an unprecedented glimpse into how humans came to be. Here, <em>Inverse</em> lists everything we’ve learned about human evolution in the past year.</p><p class="contained">While we are the only hominids to walk the Earth today, this year genomic evidence proved that the DNA of some people contains traces of Paleolithic trysts between humans and other <em>Homo</em> species, like the Neanderthals and the Denisovans. A decade ago, scientists would have been extremely skeptical. University of Buffalo evolutionary anthropologist <a href="https://gokcumenlab.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Omer Gokcumen, Ph.D.</a> tells <em>Inverse</em> that it’s only in the past five years that scientists have been able to confirm that early Sapiens didn’t only hang out with other Sapiens.</p><p class="contained"> “You can think of the world 100,000 years ago like a <em><a href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/lord-of-the-rings">Lord of the Rings</a></em> world, where different, pretty smart human-like creatures are roaming,” Gokcumen says, explaining that this situation is called interaction dynamism. “One of them would be our ancestors in Africa, but there would also be other species like Neanderthals, that we could tell were different — but not different enough to not interbreed and produce fertile offspring.”</p><p class="-last contained">This year, academics including Gokcumen published an extensive amount of research filling in the details of this <a href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/tolkien">Tolkien</a>-esque world, furthering our understanding of how our minds, bodies, and behavior came to be. Learning what it means to be human, it seems, means learning about really old humans.</p></div></div></div></section><section class="article-card flex palm-flex-column app-content"><div class="alignment alignment--top alignment--middle"><div class="alignment__body"><h2 class="-first clearfix contained">1. Ancient Spit Reveals Mysterious Human ‘Ghost’ Ancestor</h2><p class="contained">In July, Gokcumen and a team of scientists announced in <em>Molecular <a href="https://www.inverse.com/topic/biology">Biology</a> and Evolution</em> that a protein in the saliva of people from sub-Saharan Africa indicates that they carry genetic <a href="https://www.inverse.com/article/34635-ancient-humans-ghost-species-spit-analysis">evidence of an unknown hominin ancestor</a>. The saliva protein became a point of interest when University of Buffalo oral biologist and co-author Stefan Ruhl, Ph.D. compared the saliva proteins of primates and humans. Typically, these proteins look very similar. That’s why it was startling when they discovered that one protein from the sub-Saharan African population was revealed to be very different in size than the others.</p><p class="contained">This protein, known as MUC7, is thought to be the result of genetic material left over from mating between <em>Homo sapiens</em> and a ‘ghost’ species as recently as 150,000 years ago. Gokcumen believes this mysterious species was confined to Africa and split from <em>Homo sapiens</em>’ evolutionary path around three million years ago. This study is further proof that we, in Gokcumen’s polite words, “absorbed different populations that lived around us” — mating with other <em>Homo</em> species, many of which are still unidentified.</p><p class="contained">“Our study adds to this generally more colorful picture of human history, and it’s becoming very clear that it’s not as simple as we previously thought,” says Gokcumen. “It’s become very clear to researchers that even though the majority of our genome can be traced back to particular, <em>Homo sapien</em> ancestral population, there’s observable evidence that other, smaller populations made their way into our modern human gene pool.”</p><p class="-last contained"><strong><em>Read more: <a href="https://www.inverse.com/article/34635-ancient-humans-ghost-species-spit-analysis">Humans Had Sex with Ancient ‘Ghost’ Species, Spit Analysis Shows</a></em></strong></p></div></div></div></section><div class="contained newsletter-accordion scrollspy"><p class="separator__header">Article continues below</p><div class="separator newsletter-modal"><div class="newsletter-signup"><div class="newsletter-signup__intro">The revolution will not be televised.
