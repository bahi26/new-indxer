The green, red, and blue components of the colours of the countries represent the percentages for the <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, industry, and <a href="/wiki/Services_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Services sector">services sectors</a>, respectively.</div></div></div><p><b>Industry</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> of <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> or related <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> within an <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The major source of revenue of a group or company is the indicator of its relevant industry.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When a large group has multiple sources of revenue generation, it is considered to be working in different industries. Manufacturing industry became a key sector of production and labour in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> countries during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, upsetting previous <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> economies. This came through many successive rapid advances in technology, such as the production of <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>.</p><p>Following the Industrial Revolution, possibly a third of the world's economic output are derived that is from <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> industries. Many <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a> and many developing/semi-developed countries (China, India etc.) depend significantly on manufacturing industry. Industries, the countries they reside in, and the economies of those countries are interlinked in a complex web of interdependence.</p><p></p><div id="toc" class="toc"><div class="toctitle" lang="en" Here is a <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_thermal_oxidiser" class="mw-redirect" title="Regenerative thermal oxidiser">thermal oxidizer</a> during the industrial shipping process.</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg/220px-Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg/330px-Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg/440px-Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1923" data-file-height="1082" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kunda_tsemenditehas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A factory, a traditional symbol of the industrial development (a <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> factory in <a href="/wiki/Kunda,_Estonia" title="Kunda, Estonia">Kunda, Estonia</a>)</div></div></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> led to the development of factories for large-scale production with consequent changes in society.<sup id="cite_ref-More2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-More2000-3">[3]</a></sup> Originally the factories were steam-powered, but later transitioned to <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> once an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a> was developed. The mechanized <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> was introduced to assemble parts in a repeatable fashion, with individual workers performing specific steps during the process. This led to significant increases in efficiency, lowering the cost of the end process. Later <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a> was increasingly used to replace human operators. This process has accelerated with the development of the <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robot</a>.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deindustrialisation">Deindustrialisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Deindustrialisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Deindustrialisation">Deindustrialisation</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clark%27s_Sector_Model.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Clark%27s_Sector_Model.png/450px-Clark%27s_Sector_Model.png" width="450" height="269" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Clark%27s_Sector_Model.png 1.5x" data-file-width="603" data-file-height="360" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clark%27s_Sector_Model.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Colin_Clark_(economist)" title="Colin Clark (economist)">Colin Clark</a>'s sector model of an economy undergoing technological change. In later stages, the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Quaternary sector of the economy">Quaternary sector of the economy</a> grows.</div></div></div><p>Historically certain manufacturing industries have gone into a decline due to various economic factors, including the development of replacement technology or the loss of competitive advantage. An example of the former is the decline in <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriage</a> manufacturing when the <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a> was mass-produced.</p><p>A recent trend has been the migration of prosperous, industrialized nations towards a <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial society</a>. This is manifested by an increase in the <a href="/wiki/Service_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Service sector">service sector</a> at the expense of manufacturing, and the development of an information-based economy, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Informational_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Informational revolution">informational revolution</a>. In a post-industrial society, manufacturers relocate to more profitable locations through a process of <a href="/wiki/Off-shoring" class="mw-redirect" title="Off-shoring">off-shoring</a>.</p><p>Measurements of manufacturing industries outputs and economic effect are not historically stable. Traditionally, success has been measured in the number of jobs created. The reduced number of employees in the manufacturing sector has been assumed to result from a decline in the competitiveness of the sector, or the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Lean_manufacturing" title="Lean manufacturing">lean manufacturing</a> process.</p><p>Related to this change is the upgrading of the <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a> of the product being manufactured. While it is possible to produce a low-technology product with low-skill labour, the ability to manufacture high-technology products well is dependent on a highly skilled staff.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">Industrial society</a></div><p>An <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">industrial society</a> can be defined in many ways. Today, industry is an important part of most societies and nations. This occurs typically in the manufacturing sector. A labour union is an organization of workers who have banded together to achieve common goals in key areas such as wages, hours, and other working conditions. The trade union, through its leadership, bargains with the employer on behalf of union members (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rank_and_file" class="extiw" title="wikt:rank and file">rank and file</a> members) and negotiates labour contracts with employers.
